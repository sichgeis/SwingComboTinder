# Style guide for bilingual card backs

This guide defines how transcript research becomes the compact German and English teaching copy on
the back of a Swing Thing card. It complements the structural format in
[`../specs/flexible-bilingual-card-guides.md`](../specs/flexible-bilingual-card-guides.md).

The goal is not to turn a card into a complete dance lesson. A good card gives a dancer a precise,
memorable model of the figure: what changes, when it changes, where the energy goes, what Lead and
Follow each attend to, and what should remain free.

## Editorial principles

### Research broadly, then synthesize

Read every useful transcript available for the figure before drafting. Individual teachers often
emphasize different layers:

- one source may explain the basic rhythm and shape;
- another may clarify direction, axis, or variations;
- another may describe hands, frame, safety, or role-specific choices.

The card should synthesize the strongest compatible teaching points. It should not summarize the
videos one after another or reproduce one teacher's vocabulary uncritically.

Transcripts are research evidence, not unquestionable truth. Automatic captions may contain missing
words, repeated passages, wrong role names, or incorrect dance terms. Compare statements across
sources, check the visible figure metadata and existing guide, and prefer claims supported by more
than one source. When teachers describe legitimate variations, distinguish the classic version from
the broader family instead of presenting one variation as universal.

### Write the German guide first

German is the default product language and the primary editorial draft. Establish the movement model
in natural German Swing language first. Then write the English guide from that model.

The English guide is not a sentence-by-sentence translation. It should preserve the same mechanics,
directional facts, safety, and emphasis while sounding like an original English dance explanation.
The two languages may choose different sentence structure and slightly different headings.

### Prefer Swing Denglisch over forced translations

Use the vocabulary dancers are likely to hear in class and at socials. English terms are welcome
when they are more precise or more natural than a literal German substitute.

Usually keep these terms in English:

- Lead and Follow
- Connection and Frame
- Tension, Compression, Stretch, Release
- Travel
- Inside Turn, Outside Turn, Free Spin and other established move names
- Closed Position, Open Position, Side-by-Side and Tandem
- Rock Step, Triple Step, Kick Step, Bounce, Pulse and Groove
- Counts when referring to counted musical positions

German grammar may surround these terms normally: “der Lead”, “der Follow”, “die Connection”, “im
Frame”, “releasen”, “der Travel”. Use this deliberately, not decoratively. A familiar German word is
still preferable when it is equally precise: “Richtung”, “Drehung”, “Schwerpunkt”, “Körperseite”,
“Boden”, “Raum” and “Schritte” need no English replacement.

Avoid stiff literal translations such as:

- “Verbindung” when the dance concept specifically means Connection;
- “reisender Ausgang” for a traveling exit;
- “Reisebahn” when Travel or a concrete description of movement through the room is clearer;
- “Führender” and “Folgender” as replacements for Lead and Follow.

### Be spatially explicit

The guide should let a dancer reconstruct the important pathway without seeing the video. Name:

- the starting and finishing positions;
- the direction of rotation;
- whether the action travels or stays in place;
- when a direction changes;
- where Tension, Compression, or Stretch is received;
- which space Lead opens or clears;
- whether a turn is an Inside Turn or Outside Turn when that classification is useful.

Left and right are incomplete without a perspective. Write “aus Sicht des Follows nach rechts”,
“zur linken Seite des Leads”, or another explicit reference when ambiguity is possible. Do not rely
on the reader guessing whether a direction is viewed from the camera, Lead, Follow, or room.

Describe the mechanism, not merely the destination. “Der Follow dreht nach rechts” is less useful
than “Die Rotation wechselt nach links in den Tuck und released anschließend nach rechts in die
Outside Turn.”

### Explain energy without inventing force

Tension and Release should identify how energy changes through the partnership. State whether it
comes from maintained Frame, opposing body directions, continuing footwork, or changing distance.

Do not imply that the arms manufacture the movement when the body and back connection carry the
information. Words such as “push”, “pull”, “abstoßen” and “schicken” need care:

- say what body surface or connection receives the pressure;
- distinguish an elastic rebound from an active shove;
- identify the role of the floor and the dancer's own weight;
- keep shoulders, elbows and wrists free;
- make clear that the hand is a mobile connection or reference point, not the axis of a turn.

If “abstoßen” accurately describes the feeling, qualify it. For example: the Follow may feel an
Abfedern from the left side and use the floor for the Release, without pushing away through the arm.

### Separate shared mechanics from role-specific attention

Begin with the shared shape before dividing into Lead and Follow. The shared sections explain the
figure as one partnered event. Role sections then clarify responsibility:

- Lead shapes direction, timing, available space and the changing connection without forcing;
- Follow responds to the current information, maintains rhythm and balance, and does not anticipate
  a memorized destination;
- both retain agency over their own feet, axis, range and comfort.

Avoid gendered role assumptions. Lead and Follow are dance roles.

### Keep the explanation compact

Every sentence must add a movement fact, sensory cue, timing relationship, role responsibility, or
safety constraint. Remove sentences that merely restate a heading or repeat the same idea with new
metaphors.

As a working target:

- description: one sentence;
- body: roughly five to seven sections;
- section: usually one short paragraph, sometimes two;
- paragraph: usually two or three sentences;
- remember cue: one sentence or compact phrase.

This is a target, not a schema. A simple figure may need fewer sections. A subtle figure may need one
extra section. The card must remain useful when read quickly on a phone.

## Recommended content structure

The body syntax uses `## Heading` and plain paragraphs separated by blank lines. Headings belong to
the content and may vary by figure.

### Die Grundidee

Open with the shortest accurate model of the whole figure. Include the usual start and end position,
the defining change of direction or shape, and the turn or release that follows. A dancer should
understand the figure after this paragraph even if they read nothing else.

English commonly uses `## The basic idea`.

### Rhythmus und Ablauf

Place the defining actions inside the rhythm. Use Rock Step, Triple Step, Kick Step, Counts, or phrase
length only when they help the dancer locate the movement. Avoid count-by-count transcription when a
shorter relationship is enough.

English commonly uses `## Rhythm and sequence`.

### Tension und Release

Describe how the elastic relationship changes. Name Frame, Connection, Stretch, Compression,
direction changes, body surfaces and the point where pressure becomes lighter. Not every figure
needs this exact heading, but partnered mechanics should not disappear into a generic hands section.

English commonly uses `## Tension and release`.

### Als Lead

Explain what Lead communicates with the body, when space must be opened, and what the hands must not
force. Prefer concrete actions over commands such as “lead clearly”.

English commonly uses `## As lead`.

### Als Follow

Explain what information Follow answers, how axis and footwork continue, and which part must not be
anticipated. Prefer concrete attention over generic advice such as “keep balance”.

English commonly uses `## As follow`.

### Hände und Frame

Collect the remaining hand-path, elbow, shoulder, free-arm, and comfort details. Omit this section if
those facts already fit naturally into Tension/Release and role sections.

English commonly uses `## Hands and frame`.

### Optional figure-specific section

Add a dedicated section when the figure has a genuinely useful concept that does not belong in the
standard structure: a traditional image, a diagnostic question, a characteristic rhythm, a common
variation, or an established mnemonic.

Do not manufacture a catchy device for every figure. Use one when:

- it appears consistently in teaching material;
- the user supplies it from lived dance practice;
- it encodes the actual mechanics rather than merely sounding memorable.

For the Tuck Turn, “Hip – Hip – Hooray” is useful because its three accents map to Tension right,
Tension left, and Release into the Outside Turn. It therefore earns its own section and can also
serve as the remember cue. Most figures will use a more conservative remember cue made from the
existing movement vocabulary.

## Description

The description appears on both card faces, so it must work before the longer explanation. It should:

- identify the figure's defining mechanism rather than list every step;
- use one compact sentence;
- avoid claims that the body later contradicts;
- mention start/end positions only when they distinguish the figure;
- share the guide's terminology.

Tuck Turn example:

> Die Tuck Turn spannt die Connection in zwei Richtungen auf und released den Follow aus dem Tuck in
> eine Outside Turn von Closed nach Open.

English adaptation:

> Build tension through two opposing directions, receive the tuck, then release the follow into an
> outside turn from closed to open.

## Remember cue

The remember cue is the shortest useful retrieval key for the complete guide. Prefer a compact
sequence of established terms over a newly invented slogan.

Good conservative pattern:

> Richtung zeigen, Connection leicht werden lassen, auf den eigenen Füßen landen.

Use a mnemonic only when it carries real movement information. Tuck Turn example:

> Hip – Hip – Hooray: Tension nach rechts, Tension nach links, Release in die Outside Turn.

The English cue should sound natural rather than mimic German grammar:

> Hip – Hip – Hooray: tension right, tension left, release into the outside turn.

## Revision examples from the Tuck Turn

These examples show how editorial precision improved through review.

### Replace vague space with a specific shape

Too vague:

> Auf dem Rock Step öffnet sich das Paar zunächst leicht zu einer Seite.

Better:

> Auf dem Rock Step öffnet sich das Paar in eine aufgespannte Position: Aus Sicht des Follows rotiert
> der Körper nach rechts und hält dabei die Connection.

The revision identifies the shape, perspective, direction, and continuing relationship.

### Replace stiff German with established dance language

Stiff:

> Für einen reisenden Ausgang räumt der Lead die Bahn frei.

Better:

> Die eigene Position bestimmt den anschließenden Travel. Soll der Follow durch den Raum gehen, macht
> der Lead den entsprechenden Platz frei.

The revision uses familiar class vocabulary and describes the visible action without the awkward
metaphor of a journey.

### Name the turn created by the redirection

Incomplete:

> Aus dem Richtungswechsel entsteht die Drehung.

Better:

> Die Rotation wechselt nach links in den Tuck. Dort wird die Bewegung kurz aufgefangen und als
> Outside Turn wieder nach rechts released.

The revision distinguishes preparation from result and relates the figure to the established
Inside/Outside Turn vocabulary.

### Distinguish elastic response from arm force

Risky:

> Der Follow stößt sich links ab und dreht.

Better:

> Im Tuck nimmt der Follow die Compression besonders über die linke Körperseite und die Connection am
> Rücken auf. Beim Release wird die Connection leichter; der Follow nutzt den Boden und die
> gespeicherte Energie für die Drehung.

The revision preserves the felt rebound while avoiding an instruction to push with the arm.

### Remove repetition across sections

If `Die Grundidee`, `Hip – Hip – Hooray`, and `Tension und Release` all say that energy changes
direction, give each a distinct job:

- Grundidee: complete map of the figure;
- mnemonic: quick rhythmic recall;
- Tension/Release: physical source and quality of the energy.

Do not repeat the same full explanation under all three headings.

## Workflow for a new or revised guide

1. Read `figure.ts`, `notes.md`, and every file under the figure's optional `transcripts/`.
2. Record the points on which the sources agree: rhythm, start/end position, directions, pathways,
   connection changes, role responsibilities, and safety.
3. Mark teacher-specific variations, metaphors, and disputed details separately.
4. Decide which version the card describes as the basic figure. Mention variations only when they
   clarify the family without obscuring the basic action.
5. Draft the German `Die Grundidee` first.
6. Add only the sections needed to explain rhythm, mechanics, roles, hands, and any supported
   figure-specific concept.
7. Replace forced translations with established Swing Denglisch and make every direction's
   perspective explicit.
8. Cut repetition and test whether each paragraph adds new information.
9. Write or revise the German description and remember cue from the finished body.
10. Write the English guide as a natural adaptation of the same movement model.
11. Compare both languages for factual agreement, not sentence parity.
12. Preview the full back on a phone-sized card and trim anything that obscures the useful scan path.

## Final review checklist

- Have all available transcripts been read?
- Were automatic-caption errors and repeated transcript segments treated cautiously?
- Does the first section explain the whole figure compactly?
- Are start and end positions accurate where stated?
- Are left/right directions tied to an explicit perspective?
- Are Inside Turn, Outside Turn, Travel, Connection, Frame, Tension, Compression and Release used
  consistently?
- Does the explanation distinguish body-led information from hand force?
- Are Lead and Follow responsibilities concrete and non-gendered?
- Does every section add something new?
- Is an optional mnemonic supported rather than invented for novelty?
- Does the remember cue retrieve the central movement pattern?
- Does the English version preserve the mechanics while reading naturally?
- Is the guide still comfortable to scan on the back of a phone-sized card?
